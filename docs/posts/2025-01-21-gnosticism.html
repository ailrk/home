Gnosticism

January 21, 2025, en

Gnosticism was a diverse collection of religious movements and philosophical ideas that flourished in the early centuries of the Common Era, primarily alongside early Christianity. The world itself stems from the Greek term gnosis, meaning “knowledge”, but not just intellectual information; it refers to a deep, experiental insight into the nature of the divine. Central to Gnostic belief was the idea that the material world is inherently flawed or even evil, created not by the supreme, benevolent God, but by an inferior and often arrogant artisan-deity known as the Demiurge.

According to Gnostic thought, human beings contain a “diving spark” or a fragment of the true celestial realm that has become trapped within the physical body and the material universe. Salvation, therefore, is not achieved through faith, good works, or adherence to law, but through the awakening of this spark via secret knowledge. This gnosis allows the soul to recognize its true origin and ascend back tothe spriritual realm, or Pleroma, after death. Because of its emphasis on internal revelation and its rejection of the physical world-including the traditional structures of the church-gnosticism was eventually condemned as heresy by orthodox Chrsitian leaders, leading many of its original texts, such as those found at Nag Hammadi, to be hidden or destroyed for centuries.

Gnosticism flourished during the 2nd and 3rd centuries CE, emerging from the melting pot of Jewish mysticism, Greek philosopohy (particularly Platonism), and early Chrstian thought. It wasn’t a single, unified church but rather a cluster of independent groups, such as the Valentinians and Sethians, who shared a common “world-rejecting” outlook. These groups often viewed the Old testament God as the Demiurge-a lower, ignorant entity who mistakenly beleived he was the supreme creator-while the true, transcendent Father remained hidden from those without spiritual insight.

The discovery of the Nag Hammadi library in Egypt in 1945 significantly changed our understanding of these movements. Before this find, almost everything known about Gnosticism came from the writings of their opponents, the early Church Fathers like Irenaeus, who sought to debunk them. The Nag Hammadi texts, including “Aeons” (emanations of divine) and the fall of Sophia (Wisdom), whose error led to the creation of the material world. This worldview created a strict dualism between the “pneumatics” (spriritual people capable of gnosis) and the “hylics” (those bound to the material world), positioning the human struggle as a journey of remembering one’s celestial identity to escape the cycle of reincarnation or cosmic imprisonment.

Scholars often point out the striking parallels between Gnosticism and Bhddhism, particualrly regarding the idea of the world as a place of suffering and illusion. At the heart of Gnostic cosmology is the Monad, the ultimate, unknownable, and infinite source of all light and being. From this “One”, various divine emanations or Aeons flowed, forming the spiritual realm known as the Pleroma. However, through cosmic rupture-often attributed to the Aeon Sopha’s desire to know the Monad-the material world was accidentally birthed by the Demiurge. This creation was considered a “divine tragedy” because it resulted in fragments of Monad’s essence, the divine sparks, becoming imprisoned wihtin within human body.

The Gnostic path is essentially a journey of “remembering” what the soiuld has forgotten, Much like the Buddhist concept of Samsara(轮回), Gnostics belived the physical world was a trap of ignorance maintained by the Archons (servants of the Demiurge). The spark represents our true, spiritual self that is currenlty “asleep” in the material shell. Salvation is the process of Return, where the individual attain gnosis bypass the celestial jailers and ascend back to the Monad. This mirrors the Buddhist goal of Nirvana(涅槃), where practitioner pierces the veil of Maya(幻) to end the cycle of rebirth. In both traditions, the primary “sin” isn’t moral disobedience, but rather ignorance of one’s own divine or enlightened nature.

Sabbatean Frankism is often described by scholars as a “rebirth” of Gnostic ideas within a Jewish framework. While it emerged in the 17th and 18th centuries-long after the original gnostics were suppressed-it shares a fundamental structural DNA with them. The connection primarily in three radical concepts:

  1. The false god vs the true god dicotomy: Just as ancient Gnostics distinguished between the “Demigurge” and the “Monad”, Jacob Frank taught that the laws of Torah belonged to a “false” authority. He believed that to reach the true “Good God”, one had to bypass or violate the laws of the lower creators. This mirrored the Gnostic belief that the material world and its religious rules were a prison designed to keep the divine spark trapped.

  2. Redemption through sin (Antinomianism): The most famous and controversial theology of Frankism. In traditional Gnosticism, since the material world is evil, the laws governing it are seen as irrelevant or even obstructive. Frank took this into an streme: he taught that becasue the world was so corrupted, theonly way to liberate the sparks was to descend into the abyss of sin. By intentionally breaking taboos, Frankists believed they were exhausting the power of the evil realm, effectively fighting fire with fire to trigger the return to the divine source.

  3. The divine spark and the alien soul: Frankism adopted the Kabbalistic idea of Sefirot (emanations), but interpreted it through a lens that felt very Gnostic. They viewd the soul as a “foreigner” or an “alien” in this world-a diving fragment that does not belong here. The goal was not to “fix” the world through prayer or good deeds, but to engage in a spiritual war to extract the light from the darkness and return it to the Monad.

While Gnosticism was a prodcut of the late Roman Empire and Frankism a product of Englightenment-era Poland, they both represent a “rebellion of the soul” against the physical and legalistic structures of the world. They both concldue that they only way to find God is to reject the “God” that the rest of the world worship.

Frankism and Buddhism are both very similar to Gnosticism, but why the impression they give are so different? It lays on some structural differences.

  1. The Nature of the Self: The fundamental difference lies in what exactly does it mean to be liberated. Frankism is deeply essentialist. It assumes you have a soul that is literal piece of God. The goal is to rescue this specific, individual entity from the husks (kelipot) of evil. It is a rescue mission for a real self. Meanwhile Buddhism is non-essentailist. Buddhism teaches Anatta, the doctrine that there is no permanent soul or spark. Liberation isn’t about rescuing a piece of God, it’s about realizing the self you are trying to rescue never existsed in the first place.

  2. The Path (Transgression vs Discipline): Frankism is antinomian. It teaches that because the world is so broken, you must break the law to fix it. Sinning is a holy act. It is chaotic, transgressive, and often involves viotlating social and moral taboos. Buddhism, on the other hand, is generally disciplined. Even though it views the world as an Maya (illusion, 幻), it teaches that ethical conduct (Sila, 戒) is the foundatioan of progress. You don’t sin your way out of suffering, you use mindfulness and eightfold path (八正道) to extinguish the desires that cause suffering.

  3. The goal (Return vs. Extinction): For Frankists, the goal is the restoration of God’s unity. By gathering the sparks, the Frankists helps “repair” the divine, leading to a Messianic age where hidden god is finally revealed. It’s a cosmic homecoming. For Buddhism, the goal is Nirvana(涅槃), which literately means extinguishing. You don’t return to a creator, but the cessation of the fire of craving. It’s the end of the cycle of rebirth by removing the fuel (ignorance and desire) that keeps the wheel turning.
